Amplify: To make larger or greater (as in amount, importance or intensity)


Born out of a desire to provide a platform for artists of marginalized identities, the Amplify program at the Arvada Center strives to showcase a diverse slate of artistic mediums while providing the opportunity for dialogue between artists and audiences.


Now having produced four live events and six digital episodes, the Amplify program continues to pick up steam and host extraordinary artists.


“Since the beginning of Amplify as a series of online episodes, the mission of this program has been to provide a platform for expressing individual truth,” says Marcus Turner, Director of Communications and Audience Services and project manager of the Amplify series. “It is important for the Arvada Center to act only as a catalyst – the content and message comes from the curator and participants.”


Beginning with Betty Hart, award-winning actor, director, and President of the Colorado Theatre Guild, the tradition was set early on to showcase artists in a wide variety of disciplines and show how the artforms can work together. In the case of Colorado Poet Laureate Bobby LeFebre, the vision was to bring artists from the margins into the center. Encompassing everything from fashion design and silent disco to dance and documentary film, LeFebre showcased how Latinx identity forms the inspiration for expression. In a Q&A at the conclusion of his event, LeFebre asked the participants to sum up their experience in one word and one artist simply stated, “Finally.”


Representatives from the Denver American Indian Festival focused on Indigenous issues that many audience members may not have known about. From the prevalence of Native American soldiers in wartime to the numerous cases of murdered and missing Indigenous women, these issues were not raised in a lecture but rather through traditional Native American dances.


Denver arts icon Cleo Parker Robinson hosted a conversation with two dancers from her company. In their conversation, the artists described the importance of safety in a rehearsal space. With a long history of focusing on issues of race in her choreography Parker Robinson and her dancers emphasized how important these conversations remain in order to cultivate growth.


As we look to the future, the Amplify program will continue to explore truth, provide opportunities for dialogue, and bring artists to the Arvada Center whose story may not have been told before. In addition, all past Amplify events can be viewed on the Arvada Center Vimeo page.
